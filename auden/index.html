Wystan Hugh Auden

1907-1973
Yorkshire, England
English Poet • Political Activist • Lover of People

Reputation

WH Auden was renowned across Britain during the Great Depression and was acclaimed as the successor to T.S. Eliot after Eliot's death in 1965. Auden had a gift to raise the spirits of the people and challenge old ideas with progressive Modernist manuscript. He was able to reach such a wide audience thanks to his mastery of both prose and poetry during the 1930s-1940s, appealing to everyone who opposed war.
His popularity grew as he wrote plays alongside Christopher Isherwood, both were homosexual. This was not uncommon among young writers, however. Feelings of isolation and being forced to lead a double life sparked the creative minds of many modern poets. The Modern era was the perfect opportunity for these bold young authors to freely express pain, anxiety, and longing from hiding who they were.

Inspiration

Auden attended Oxford University and afterwards moved to Berlin where he grew to love the German language. In Spain, he wrote of his encounter with a country englufed in intense political conflict.
Auden adopted a love of reading at a young age from poets such as William Blake, Emily Dickinson, Robert Frost, Thomas Hardy, and Gerard Manley Hopkins. As a writer, Auden loved love. Many of his poems detailed youthful love, losing love to death, and overcoming barriers in the pursuit of love.

Legacy

Auden's prose and poetry inspired writers many young writers from different backgrounds to form the "Auden Group". Among the first generation members of the Auden Group were
• C. Day Lewis who was named Poet Laureate in 1968
• Louis MacNeice, an Irish playwright
• Stephen Spender, a Poet Laureate who printed the first collection of Auden’s poems in 1928 by hand.
• T.S. Eliot who published Auden's work and inspired Auden simultaneuously.
• W.B. Yeats for whom Auden wrote "In Memory of W. B. Yeats"

After his death, Auden's writing resurfaced and his popularity soared after his poem "Funeral Blues" was tearfully recited in the 1994 movie "Four Weddings and a Funeral".
